What she was really saying
Read this when the urge is loudest.
Her fear

"You don't need me? I'll start crying now. I got a heart attack."

She wasn't being dramatic. The moment she felt you pulling away — her body responded. Her chest tightened. She was telling you: your choices physically hurt me. Not as guilt. As truth.

The plea

"Baba please meri baat suno. Please beta."

She dropped everything she had — pride, composure, authority — and just begged. A person who loves you without condition is the only person who begs like this. She wasn't commanding you. She was surrendering to you, hoping you'd respond.

Her uncertainty about herself

"I don't know where I'll be… But please take care of yourself. Stop all these."

She didn't know how much time she had. She was facing her own uncertainty — and her first thought was still you. Not herself. You. That's what love looks like when it's completely selfless.

Her last will

"If I die — my last words to you: Allah is your protector. Please leave smoking."

Think about that. If she had one breath left, one final sentence to give you — she chose this. Not "I love you." Not "be good." She chose: leave smoking. Because she knew this was the thing standing between you and the life you deserve.

Your worth in her eyes

"You are important. You should be happy. I want you to be okay."

She didn't say this to flatter you. She said it because she genuinely saw something in you that you couldn't see in yourself. She was telling you: your life has value beyond what you think. Don't waste it on something that slowly destroys you.

Her life's effort

"See, I tried my best in my life to guide you. Don't destroy my efforts. But I don't want you to smoke — never ever again. I can't leave you easily, beta. My efforts — Allah knows."

She was asking you to honor a lifetime of her care. Everything she gave you — time, prayers, worry, sleepless nights — it all lived in that one sentence. Don't destroy my efforts. Every cigarette is a rejection of everything she built in you.

She tried to be your peace

"Beta listen. See. Be calm. You have Jannah. You have Allah with you. Please remember Allah. Remember me. But never be sad."

Even in her panic, she stopped and tried to calm you. She was the one in pain and she still said: be calm, never be sad. She wanted to hold your heart together from wherever she was. That's a mother's love — it puts you first even when she is breaking.

Your future

"Who told you to forget me? Prove to Allah that you deserve the best things. I will remember you in my sujood. You'll get a beautiful woman — a righteous woman. What's the problem? Just ask Allah."

She wasn't telling you to move on. She was telling you to be worthy of what's coming. She was making dua for your future wife while worrying about you in this moment. She saw your whole life ahead — and she wanted all of it to be good.

She saw through your walls

You told her: I don't want anyone.

She said: "You need Ummer."

She didn't argue. She didn't lecture. She just quietly said the truth — that you need yourself. She knew your self-destruction better than you did. And she called it by your own name.

She would have walked to you

"Main udhar rahti toh paas ch aati."

If I was there, I would have come to you. She wasn't saying this poetically. She meant it literally. She would have gotten up, crossed whatever distance, and stood in front of you — just to make you listen. Distance was the only thing stopping her.

She knew you were busy — and waited anyway

"Manje malum kaisa hota karko. Meri baat sundalo — ek baar. Please karo manje call. Honde manje bhi baat karo."

She knew you were on a call with your sister. She understood. And she still waited — because what she had to say couldn't wait. She wasn't being impatient. She was scared. Scared you'd reach for a cigarette and she wouldn't be there to stop it.

The wait

"Main wait karryu. Main baat karna chahti hoon tumse. Ummer mere paas baat karryu. Please beta — baat karo."

Message after message. She sat there, phone in hand, watching for a reply that didn't come. She didn't give up. She didn't get angry. She just kept saying: I'm still here. Talk to me. That kind of patient love is rare. It deserves to be honored.

Her one and only request

"Yetta request karna? Main yetta samjhaaryu honde chodo nakko cigarettes."

Is this my only request? She was saying: I don't ask much of you. I never asked for money, status, or success. I am asking for one thing. This one thing. Leave this. That's all.

The most important thing she ever said to you

"I see me in you."

This is not about cigarettes anymore. She looked at you and saw herself — her struggles, her strength, her spirit — living inside you. She wasn't just trying to save you from smoking. She was trying to protect the part of her that lives on through you.

When you light up, you're not just hurting yourself. You're slowly erasing something of her.

When you put it down — you carry her forward.

Every time the urge hits — this is what's on the other side of it. Her words. Her wait. Her love. Her last request. Her in you.
